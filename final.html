Classsification of Heart Disease¶

Introduction¶

One of the most common health conditions throughout the world is heart disease. Atherosclerosis, manifesting as vessel narrowing or vessel stenosis, occurs due to an accumulation of fat within the blood vessels of the heart. Narrowing of blood vessels usually implies a higher risk of some kinds of heart disease (Malota et al., 2018), and this vessel stenosis is likely related to several factors such as age, blood pressure, and serum cholesterol levels.

Heart problems are common in older adults due to aging arteries. Aging change in arteries usually presents as a reduced elasticity on the large blood vessels related to atherosclerosis resulting in high blood pressure. Aging also results in other health risks, such as limits to blood flow and decreased heart chamber capacity (Heart Health and Aging, n.d.). Research on aging arteries and heart by Lakatta and Levy (2003) demonstrates that although heart-related aging may not directly cause heart disease, it leads to increased vulnerability and weakness of the heart, increasing the risk of heart disease.

Blood pressure can reflect the health of blood vessels. High blood pressure reduces the supply of blood and oxygen to the heart, and insufficient oxygen levels in the blood may cause heart attacks and heart failure (High Blood Pressure Symptoms, Causes, and Problems | cdc.gov, 2021). According to the research on the relation between blood pressure and coronary heart disease in different ages by Franklin et al. (2001), diastolic blood pressure is considered an efficient predictor for this heart disease in people under 50 years old, and other types of blood pressure are also associated with the risks of heart disease in patients with other ages.

On the other hand, serum cholesterol values can indicate potential blood flow restrictions in blood vessels (Warning Signs and Symptoms of Heart Disease, n.d. & Malota et al., 2018). Law et al. (1994) conclude a possible strong and positive relationship between the value of serum cholesterol and the risk of heart disease by investigating the effect of lower serum cholesterol levels on the probability of heart disease. Law's team provides details about the benefit of lowering serum cholesterol levels in people above 40 years old. A 10% decrease in serum cholesterol will drop the probability of ischaemic heart disease by a range from approximately 20% to 50% depending on age.

It seems that the patient's age, resting blood pressure, and serum cholesterol level are closely relevant to heart disease. So, what is the predictive value of these three variables on vessel stenosis? To answer the question, this project will conduct a classification prediction on the vessel stenosis status with a random dataset Reprocessed Hungarian (Machine Learning Repository, 1988) which contains all the required variables above.

Model Setup¶

First we will create the recipe for our classification model with our actual value being vessicle narrowing (num), and the predicors being age, chest pain type (cp), resting blood pressure (trestbps), and serum cholesterol (chol). Then, we will scale and center all predictors to ensure that euclidean distance is calculated properly across variables.

In [141]:
# Create recipe to predict num based on age, trestbps, and chol
heart_recipe <- recipe(num ~ age + trestbps + chol, data = heart_training) |>
    step_scale(all_predictors()) |>
    step_center(all_predictors())

Now to make the specification and workflow objects for our model using 5-fold cross validation.

In [142]:
# Create vfold for cross-validation
heart_vfold <- vfold_cv(heart_training, v = 5, strata = num)

# Create model specification set to tune for an optimal K value
knn_spec <- nearest_neighbor(weight_func = "rectangular", neighbors = tune()) |>
  set_engine("kknn") |>
  set_mode("classification")

# Create a tibble for k-vals to test
k_vals <- tibble(neighbors = 1:15)

# Get model resuklts
knn_results <- workflow() |>
    add_recipe(heart_recipe) |>
    add_model(knn_spec) |>
    tune_grid(resamples = heart_vfold, grid = k_vals) |>
    collect_metrics()

# Filter for accuracy only
accuracies <- knn_results |>
    filter(.metric == "accuracy")

In [149]:
# Create tuned specification
knn_spec_tuned <- nearest_neighbor(weight_func = "rectangular", neighbors = 3) |>
    set_engine("kknn") |>
    set_mode("classification")

# Create fit model on training data
knn_fit <- workflow() |>
    add_recipe(heart_recipe) |>
    add_model(knn_spec_tuned) |>
    fit(data = heart_training)

Discussion¶

Our code first went about tidying the data, converting values and integers into their real world meaning, such as 0 and 1 to female and male. We also removed invalid values, replacing them with NA before removing them from the dataset entirely. After splitting our data set its testing and training sections, we attempted a preliminary examination of the variables and if there is any relationship between them. First, we removed individuals without abnormal ECG readings before trying to find a relationship between abnormality type and age, finding a slight positive correlation as individuals increase in age. Next, we attempted to see if there is a relationship between serum cholesterol, resting blood pressure and chest pain type, however our scatter plot revealed a lack of any notable relationship between these factors.

With preliminary examination out of the way, we then moved on to develop our KNN classification model. The model would be used to predict num, a binary integer which essentially represents whether or not an individual has heart disease, with 0 representing <50% vessicle narrowing (a negative diagnosis) and 1 representing >50% narrowing (a positive diagnosis). The predictive factors we used were age, chest pain type, resting blood pressure and serum cholestoral levels. To determine the ideal K value, we plotted model accuracy against K value, finding that K = 3 being ideal due to its high accuracy and efficiency. Our resulting model has a ~78% accuracy rate, and upon inspection using a confusion matrix was found to be more likely to predict a patient had heart disease than the opposite.

Unfortunately, we are unable to develop an absolutely reliable classification model based on the limited conditions of our data. Thus, the current model is unsiotable for predicting whether or not a patient has heart disease in any formal setting, such as a hospital or for the sake of public health policy

However, after evaluating the model using testing data, we found that it had an accuracy rate of approximately 80%. While this accuracy rate is insufficient for use in a hospital setting due to a 20% chance of misdiagnosis, the model could still provide valuable assistance to individuals concerned about their heart health, especially those with a family history of heart disease. Research has shown that people with a family history of heart disease are more likely to experience cardiovascular disease themselves (Lloyd-Jones et al., 2004). Thus, they need to be careful about their cardiovascular health in order to prevent heart disease, and our model could serve as a helpful tool for them. This is not only beneficial to individuals who are prone to heart disease, as the disease has a high mortality rate (Benjamin et al., 2019), but everyone should pay attention to their cardiovascular health. Some people may experience strokes, cardiac arrests, or other cardiac events without realizing they had underlying heart problems. After a regular physical examination, they could input their information into our model. If the model classifies them as having heart disease, they should go back to the hospital and seek a more thorough examination. However, it is important to note that our current model is not perfect, as there are numerous causes of heart disease and the predictors should be adjusted based on individual circumstances.

As discussed above, the findings from our KNN classification model, although not perfect, could have a variety of impacts on personal and public health levels. As an initial screening tool, the model can help individuals concerned about their heart health identify potential risks and encourage them to seek further medical examination, thus promoting early detection and timely intervention. Moreover, the model could raise public awareness about the importance of monitoring heart health, particularly among those with a family history of heart disease. This increased awareness might lead to healthier lifestyle choices and improved cardiovascular health among the general population. Additionally, the model could inform personalized risk assessments and tailored prevention strategies for individuals, ultimately reducing the burden of heart disease on both patients and the healthcare system.

The model could be made more accurate by increasing the amount of data avaialble for developing it, as well as the number of predicting variables. For example, do the variables chosen in this project apply to everyone? or whether different variables are required for different populations. Since we were required to use the KNN classification model for this project, is it possible that other statistical models may be more suitable for detecting heart disease? How can we determine which model is superior? Future research should investigate the potential impact of other variables on heart disease development, such as lifestyle factors (e.g., smoking), genetic predispositions (e.g., diabetes), and environmental factors. Exploring the model's performance across different populations or demographic groups could reveal the need for specific factors to be considered for various age groups, ethnicities, or genders. Furthermore, comparing the KNN classification model with other predictive models may help determine the best approach in terms of accuracy, interpretability, and ease of use. Overall, this project may help to develop more specific and focused questions about heart disease and its risk factors, and future research should address these aspects to advance the prevention, early detection, and management of heart diseases.
